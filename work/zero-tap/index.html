Zero Suggest on Google Maps
2015
My role
Visual, interaction and UX design. I worked closely with a researcher and manager to drive this as a UX owned project, stemming from passed and yet to be addressed research. I created the various UI patterns, tested them with a researcher and worked with my PM to define, shop, and ship this against other roadmap odds, now used as a suggest library for other Maps efforts.
  • UX lead
  • Visual design
  • UX
  • Interaction design
  • Prototyping
Challenge
People don't know they can find things without typing
Local Search query traffic for near me (food, gas, atm) had increased ~15% since 2014. We also found that comeptitors such as Apple Maps and CityMapper were solving issues for multiple use cases within each app and we weren't. We weren't surfacing top needs early enough during search and it was a challenge for those abroad to formulate the right queries. Half of our actions were covered up by the keyboard and easy to miss. Iconography also didn't match what we had on the basemap.

Opportunity + use cases
It was important to increase visibility for both driving + walking cases
We knew that we had the data to infer a mean wait time average for over 85% of restaurants listed on Google. Other verticals needed higher confidence rates to be considered.

User flows
We wanted to make these shortcuts easy + predictable to locate
After a number of prototypes and user studies, we found that most did not care for the swipe method as it hid too many of the options, and other UI patterns just didn't align to our base map and other on-going efforts.
Explorations + visual design

Outcome
Leading with iconography resulted in stronger base map + brand parity
In the end, we found that showing all categorical options to users at once was the best path forward. Most of our top categories (gas, atm, pharmacy), were frequently triggered in driving mode. Most didn't like not knowing how many options were in a swipe view and at the same time having to traverse a long line of options until you reach the end. Thus, in order to save space, we placed all categories into one card that could expand and close.

Take-aways + Next steps
Even with lots of user testing, placement + visual design may shift over time
As a constant trial period of evolving maps, interaction and visual design is moving to assist users in new ways, that may require UI to move and adapt to framework level changes.
