The Local team needed to align to Search's new design language

Quick shout out to everyone who made this project possible.

You know that business type place page on Google Maps you probably visit at least once a day? -- that's my team. We own the Local Search framework between Search and Maps. And it was time we caught up to brand-aligning initiatives, touching everything from dining and hospitals to airports and hotels across every platform.

I worked with over 20 designers, engineers and product leads on the overall framework including navigation, imagery and actions

My role was to work with the various Local Search teams between SF,Â NYC, ZRH, TOK and SYD and marry the majority of the visual design revs and explorations coming from various designers and teams and align on search's interaction patterns, alongside our own, primarily through extensive and rapid prototyping and visual design proposals against those needs.

The goal was to take research and validated system parts such as navigation, imagery and actions, and stress test them against local needs -- including dining, salons and hotel use cases. The featured patterns and interactions you see below are areas I led and contributed in defining and building out. We also worked closely with the Driving and Navigation teams and leveraged Search's system design team to keep things consistent.

This effort intended to set the stage for modernizing three types of Local Search Â -- Categorical, Chains and Navigational Search (essentially place listings across Maps and Search). We started with our own and existing projects, reshaped their roadmaps, aligned with various local teams, eg shopping, transactions, imagery and UGC. Chains and Categorical Search were already so embedded into Core Search's Framework, it was getting attention already, but Nav needed some serious work.

We learned that not everything fit the one size. Actions, imagery and navigation patterns had to break in order to meet the various local needs including transactions and driving. Some platforms had different use cases and needed to be catered to. For example, iOS and Android place sheets had a larger driving audience and needed directions to take on a bolder visual design variation then that of what Search provided. Same went for free scrolling imagery, containment models and cards, and tabbed navigation design.
