Defining a UX framework scaling between 350M places across Google

The Local team needed to align to Search's new design language

Google's local search experiences are super iconic and well known within the core search world. Over 1B DAU's, with extensive international coverage and ultimately supported by 5 timezones of designers, engineers and project managers. This effort also requires 3+ UX program managers and several UX managers to wrangle support between locales.

Also, supporting 3 types of Local Search intents -- Categorical Search, Chains Search and Navigational Search (essentially place listings across Maps and Search). We started with our own and existing projects, reshaped their roadmaps, aligned with various local teams, eg shopping, transactions, imagery and UGC. Chains and Categorical Search were already so embedded into Core Search's Result Framework, it was getting attention already, but Nav needed some serious work. This project had around 7 core designers (NYC and SF) working even closer to define and divvy up goals, versus and or in alignment with near and long term work in mind.

My role was to work with the various Local Search teams between SF, NYC, ZRH, TOK and SYD and marry the majority of the visual design revs and explorations coming from various designers and teams and align on search's interaction patterns, alongside our own, primarily through extensive and rapid prototyping and visual design proposals against those needs. The goal was to take research and validated system parts such as navigation, imagery and actions, and stress test them against local needs -- including dining, salons and hotel use cases. We also worked closely with the Driving team and leveraged Search's system design team to keep things consistent.

We learned that not everything fit the one size. Actions, imagery and navigation patterns had to break in order to meet the various local needs including transactions and driving. Some platforms had different use cases and needed to be catered to them. For example, iOS and Android place sheets had a larger driving audience and needed directions to take one a bolder visual design variation then that of what Search provided. Same went for free scrolling imagery, containment models and cards, and tabbed navigation design.
