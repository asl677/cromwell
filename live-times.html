Challenge

Users don't now how lively places are until they get there

With this launch, we aimed to augment the Popular Times histogram by overlaying the place's real time busyness, thus allowing the user to simultaneously see what's typical for the place at this time, and what's actually happening at the current time.

An MVP didn't allow us to redesign our graph or radically change the placepage. This was a fairly set of minimum requirements to start getting feedback on the data. I teamed up with a super talented motion designer to deliver a v1 experience. We then looked at existing and competitive patterns to help determine next steps.

Research

People were already trying to use Popular Times for live info - but usually for restaurants

A lot of folks were already mistaking usual popular times for real-time. So, we felt appending to that mental model was a good place to start.

We needed to provide enough contrast between usual and real-time data, so that people start to learn there's a massive difference between the two. But how do we do this in a way that scales for every place, amongst other constraints?

We looked at some other products in the space, as well as patterns within our own ecosystem.

Explorations

Color + verbiage played bias roles in the perception of the graph or anything rendered on it

Research told us that while red tested the best in terms of awareness, it created a sense of negativity across places and made users feel it wasn't a good time to visit. When in reality, a place being busy has nothing to do with wait or suggesting it isn't a good time to go, it's just how many people are there. Popularity doesn't actually mean awful, and in fact could mean - 'Hey, go now!'. How might we overlap these two pieces of data?

We didn't have bandwidth to re-invent the graph entirely. But overlaying color and text was a viable option.

Ultimately, we decided that pink plus animation would be the best direction forward. All while other colors like white against yellow didn't pass accessibility.

Findings

Color usage was sensitive

First off, shades of blue were reserved for other features of Maps. That, and red, yellow, green created a biased impression of a place's busyness. Red meant bad, green meant good and so on, which isn't actually accurate.

A new graph was too much work

To redesign the entire graph to work with this new feature was too much work and didn't exactly align to data viz across Search's evolving design system. We kept it simple and felt some sort over overlay would still be intuitive.

Live resonated better than Now

So many strings. Verbiage was sensitive and needed to be broadly conveyed. I worked with a writer to shape these across busyness cases - below, same as and above average busyness volumes.

Why a pink overlay scaled the best

In user studies, most grokked the fact that it wasn't a negative or overtly positive color at glance. Most mistook red for a bad time to visit a place - so we needed an urgent, consistent color instead of many.

This allowed for us to use the existing graph UI, and still passed accessibility also that could be used between the various cases and strings to illustrate how busy a place currently is.
